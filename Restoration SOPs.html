Restoration: The Blueprint for Integrity

Welcome to Grace Community Church. We describe our community as a mosaic—a collection of broken pieces that God is fitting together to create something beautiful.

The Restorative Process is our pathway for moving from the isolation of "hiding behind curtains" to the freedom of being fully loved and known. This document serves as a comprehensive guide, consolidating standard operating procedures, spiritual frameworks, and leadership roles to support sexual integrity and healing within a "Church Without Curtains."

 

Part 1: Standard Operating Procedure for Online Pornography - The Struggler's Blueprint

 

Objective: To move from the trauma of being "caught" (Discovery) to the freedom of "choosing to tell" (Disclosure) by implementing physical, emotional, and spiritual boundaries.

Phase 1: Immediate Stabilization (The External Brakes)

When we are struggling with a habit like phone or pornography addiction, our brain’s "biological brakes" are often overwhelmed by the "dopamine fire hose." Before we can do deep soul work, we need to create a safe environment and allow the neurological system to reset.

●      Bedroom Boundaries: Remove all internet-connected devices (phones, tablets, laptops) from the bedroom. The bedroom is reserved exclusively for rest and real-world intimacy.

●      Filter Architecture (Digital Friction): Install reputable blockers or accountability software (e.g., Covenant Eyes, Canopy). These act as "speed bumps" to give your prefrontal cortex time to engage and choose truth over an impulse.

●      Analog Resets: Designate a specific 24-hour period each week to remain entirely offline. This resets dopamine sensitivity and prevents "mindless scrolling."

●      Scripture Focus: Psalm 119:37 – "Turn my eyes from looking at worthless things; and give me life in your ways."

Phase 2: Truth-Telling (The Disclosure Protocol)

We believe that shame is a curtain we hang over our hearts to hide our true selves. Healing is impossible in the dark.  There is distinction between shame rooted in personal sin and shame resulting from being sinned against.

Shame from personal sin is often described as an "architecture of hiding," where individuals maintain a "curtain" of pretense to conceal their transgressions. This hiddenness—whether from rebellion, missing the mark, or being "bent out of shape"—leads to a state of "low-grade misery" and profound physical and emotional exhaustion. Personal transgression, such as David’s affair in Psalm 51, requires a "clean" heart.  The path to a clean heart (ṭāhôr) in this context requires confession, defined as "truth-telling," where the individual agrees with God's perspective on their inner life to find freedom from guilt.

Shame from sins committed against an individual, such as trauma or abuse, involves "things hidden because of shame" that are not the fault of the person who was harmed. This creates a sense of being "dirty," rejected, or "bound up in bitterness." Healing comes through renouncing these hidden things and the lies associated with them, effectively breaking any "spiritual attachment." A critical step is forgiveness, so that the survivor is no longer "chained to the past."

Whether shame stems from personal choice or the actions of others, the ultimate goal is the state of being ṭāhôr—being morally pure, undefiled, and free from guilt. This transformation is a divine act where God removes a "heart of stone" (representing resistance or stubbornness) and replaces it with a "heart of flesh" that is sensitive and responsive to grace. This allows an individual to move from the "shadows" into a community where they can be fully loved and fully known.

 

●      Identify a Safe Circle: Find a mentor or care team that operates under three non-negotiable rules: No Judgment, No Fixing, and Absolute Confidentiality (Safety).

●      Full Disclosure vs. Trickle-Truth: Voluntarily pull down the "curtain" by naming the struggle clearly. "Discovery" is being caught (which leads to more hiding). "Disclosure" is choosing to tell the truth yourself. Tell the truth to get healthy, not just to get out of trouble.

●      Renounce the Sales Pitch: Identify the lie you are believing (e.g., "I can have intimacy without vulnerability") and replace it with the load-bearing truth of your identity in Christ.

●      Scripture Focus: James 5:16 – "Therefore, confess your sins to one another and pray for one another, that you may be healed."

Phase 3: Root Analysis (The Wound Assessment)

Pornography and digital numbing are often "release valves" for internal pressure caused by stress, loneliness, or past wounds. We must look at the "soil" where the addiction grew.

●      Pressure Cooker Check: Identify the "HALT" triggers—Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired—that drive you to numb out.

●      The Debt List (Forgiveness): List the people you resent. Unforgiveness creates "digital static" that makes the screen feel like the only source of comfort. We guide you through explicitly canceling the debt they owe you. Releasing this debt lowers the internal pressure so you no longer need the "digital bandage."

●      Scripture Focus: Matthew 18:27 – "And out of pity for him, the master of that servant released him and forgave him the debt."

Phase 4: Constructing the Trellis (Rule of Life)

A vine that grows on the ground will rot; it needs a structure to lean on. We help you build a 3-Circle Plan to protect your growth and ensure you are not just a "lab rat" reacting to triggers, but the "author" of your life.

●      Inner Circle: What you are quitting. The specific behaviors you are renouncing (e.g., viewing pornography, secret browsing).

●      Middle Circle: Your warning signs and slippery slopes (e.g., isolating when stressed, being on your phone alone after 11 PM).

●      Outer Circle: "Analog Joys" and healthy, life-giving activities (e.g., physical exercise, real-world hobbies, serving at church).

Phase 5: Mission Shift (From Consumer to Protector)

The final goal of restoration is to have Surplus Value—the extra emotional and spiritual energy you gain when you are no longer wasting your life hiding secrets.

●      Protector of the Sandbox: Shift from being a consumer of people (viewing them as products) to a protector of your family and the next generation.

●      Repairer of the Ruins: Your story of freedom becomes a blueprint that helps others in our city find their way out from behind their own curtains.

Part 2: Standard Operating Procedure for Betrayal Trauma - The Spouse's Blueprint

Objective: To provide a sanctuary and stabilizing framework for the spouse who has experienced the trauma of discovery or disclosure, moving from the initial "explosion" toward personal safety and restored agency.

Phase 1: Immediate Stabilization (Establishing the Shelter)

In the wake of betrayal, your brain is likely in a "fight, flight, or freeze" state. Our first priority is to move you from a state of emergency to a state of safety.

●      Prioritize Self-Care over Investigation: Stop the "detective work." Constantly searching behind the "curtains" of his devices keeps your brain in a state of high-alert trauma. Focus on basics: sleep, hydration, and nutrition.

●      Define Physical Boundaries: Establish exactly what you need to feel safe in your home right now (e.g., sleeping in a separate room, requiring digital transparency).

●      The Safe Circle: Seek circles of women who operate under the rules of No Judgment, No Fixing, and Absolute Safety.

●      Scripture Focus: Psalm 46:1 – "God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble."

Phase 2: Naming the Reality (The Truth Assessment)

Betrayal creates a "shattering" of your story. The restorative process helps you pick up the pieces and see them clearly.

●      Dismantle the Shame: Recognize that your partner’s struggle is a biological and spiritual "choke-hold" on their life. It is not a reflection of your worth, your beauty, or your adequacy.

●      Disclosure over Discovery: Require a transition where your partner voluntarily brings the truth into the light (Disclosure) rather than making you hunt for it (Discovery).

●      Scripture Focus: Isaiah 54:4 – "Fear not, for you will not be ashamed; be not confounded, for you will not be disgraced."

Phase 3: Processing the Wound (Lowering the Pressure)

The intense internal pressure and pain of betrayal must be released healthily so it doesn't manifest as destructive bitterness.

●      Practice Lament: Give yourself permission to be angry and grieved.

●      The Debt Assessment: Betrayal creates a massive debt. Forgiveness is not about saying what happened was okay, nor is it "fixing" them. It is a spiritual transaction to release the debt to God so it stops rotting your own soul from the inside out.

Phase 4 & 5: Your Personal Trellis & Legacy Shift

You need a "trellis" for your own life that does not depend on your husband’s choices or progress.

●      Build Your 3-Circle Plan: Define your non-negotiables (Inner Circle), your warning signs of emotional exhaustion (Middle Circle), and the "Analog Joys" that bring you life—like real friendships, exercise, and prayer (Outer Circle).

●      Surplus Value & Protection: As you heal, you gain the surplus emotional strength to lead your children with wisdom. You shift from a state of betrayal to becoming a Protector of the Sandbox, ensuring the curtains stay down in your family for good.

 

Part 3: Freedom Framework & Restorative Prayer

The Steps to Freedom in Christ

The Steps to Freedom in Christ is a structured spiritual process to dismantle "spiritual strongholds." It focuses on bringing hidden areas into the light to break their power, moving an individual from the identity of an "addict" or "victim" to a "New Creation" and "Author" of their life.

Mentor’s Truth-Telling Checklist

Used in Safe Circles to facilitate honesty and cut through pretense:

●      Counterfeit vs. Real: Are you ready to renounce the "God Machine" of the screen and the false comfort it provides?

●      Deception: What specific lie of the algorithm (e.g., "Intimacy without risk") have you believed, and are you ready to expose it?

●      Forgiveness: Who is on your "Debt List"? Are you ready to cancel what they owe you to lower your internal pressure?

●      Bondage & Disclosure: Are you choosing Disclosure (voluntarily telling the truth today) over Discovery (waiting to be caught)?

●      Identity: Can you recite the "Who I Am in Christ" list? (e.g., "I am a temple of God," "I am significant, secure, and loved").

Restorative Prayer: The Internal Renovation

While small groups break the silence, Restorative Prayer is a direct, confidential encounter with God to heal deep, invisible wounds.

●      For the Struggler: A private session with two trained team members to "recognize and release" the roots of shame or insignificance. It is a space to hear God replace the label of "addict" with your true identity. It rebuilds the mind's internal brakes.

●      For the Betrayed Spouse: A spiritual "trauma room" to process the shattering of trust. It allows you to hear God's voice over the "digital static" of betrayal, reclaiming your authority as a protector of your home.

In a "church without curtains," recovery thrives in community. However, different roles provide different types of architectural support. It is crucial that these roles are filled by different people.

Why Must They Be Separate?

1.     Specialization: One manages the urgent tactical needs (Did you look at porn today?), while the other manages the important heart transformation (Who do you need to forgive?).

2.     Preventing "Reporting Shame": If a Mentor also receives daily software reports, a "slip" can make the participant feel they failed their spiritual guide, leading to "trickle-truth" and new curtains of pretense. Separating them creates a safe space to discuss the feelings of failure without technical judgment.

3.     Expanding the Circle: Ecclesiastes 4:12 reminds us that "a threefold cord is not quickly broken." Utilizing both roles expands your load-bearing relationships during moments of high pressure.

 

 

Part 5: Standard Operating Procedure for Managing Disclosures

Objective: To equip mentors, care team members, and church leaders to handle disclosures of sexual sin with grace, ensuring the participant moves from isolation to safety without experiencing "reporting shame" or judgment.

Your role is not to "fix" people or manage their behavior. Your role is to build a "Church Without Curtains"—an environment where the truth can be told without fear.

Phase 1: The Immediate Response (When the Curtain is Pulled Down)

When a participant bravely tells a secret for the first time, your initial reaction will either build trust or cause them to hang the curtain back up.

  • Don't Panic: Stay calm, steady, and empathetic. Do not show shock or disappointment.
  • Thank Them: The very first thing you should say is: "Thank you for being brave enough to tell the truth. That took incredible strength."
  • Validate the Step: Remind them that pulling down the curtain and choosing disclosure is the only way to break the neurological and spiritual choke-hold. Celebrate their honesty.

Phase 2: Directing the Conversation (Focusing on the Roots)

The goal of a disclosure conversation is heart healing, not gathering evidence.

  • Focus on the "Why": Do not ask for graphic details about their slip or their behavior. Instead, ask about the feeling behind the secret: "What hunger, pain, or pressure were you trying to hide in that moment?"
  • Reinforce the Three Rules: Remind them immediately that they are in a safe circle governed by three strict rules:
    1. No Judgment: We are all fighting a system designed to hijack our brains. We meet failures with grace, not condemnation.
    2. No Fixing: Do not give a laundry list of advice or try to solve their problem on the spot. Your primary job is to listen and let them be "known and loved."
    3. Absolute Safety (Confidentiality): Reassure them that what is said in the circle stays in the circle.

Phase 3: The Leader's Posture

  • The Leader's Heart: You cannot lead others where you are not willing to go. Be a "truth-teller" yourself. When you are honest about your own struggles or your own need for God's comfort, you give the group permission to drop their own curtains.
  • Guide the Trellis: If they are ready, gently help them update their 3-Circle Plan. Help them identify new "slippery slopes" (Middle Circle) that led to the disclosure, and encourage them to lean into their "analog joys" (Outer Circle).

Phase 4: Escalation Protocol (Disclosures Involving Minors)

While Grace Community Church is committed to being a safe place for healing, the physical and emotional safety of children supersedes all confidentiality agreements. If a disclosure involves the viewing, downloading, or distribution of images of children (Child Sexual Abuse Material - CSAM), the standard Care Team process must immediately stop.

  • Halt the Conversation: If an individual begins to disclose involvement with underage material, stop them gently but firmly. State clearly: "I need to stop you right there. For the safety of children, our church policy requires us to report any involvement with underage images to the authorities. I cannot keep this confidential."
  • Mandatory Reporting: Contact local law enforcement (or a national cybercrimes tip line) immediately to report the disclosure. Do not investigate this yourself; let the authorities handle it.
  • Leadership Escalation: Immediately notify the Senior Pastor and the church's legal counsel or elder board.
  • Boundary Enforcement: The individual must be immediately removed from any volunteer roles, especially those involving minors, and they will be referred to professional legal and psychological services outside the church's pastoral care structure until the legal process is resolved.

Phase 5: Volunteer Roles and The Healing Sabbatical (Non-Minor Disclosures)

If an individual discloses a struggle with adult pornography (no involvement with minors or illegal material) but currently volunteers in a children’s or youth ministry, the situation should be handled with a focus on grace and recovery rather than punishment:

  • Validate Their Honesty: Remind them that a leader who is honest about their struggle is vastly safer than a leader who is hiding. Emphasize that disclosure is the first step toward lasting integrity.
  • The Healing Sabbatical: It is a best practice to ask the individual to take a temporary, grace-filled break (a sabbatical) from serving with minors. This is not a punitive measure; it is an architectural one.
  • Lowering the Pressure: Frame the break positively. Explain: "To lead children well, you need 'Surplus Value.' Right now, your energy needs to be spent on healing your roots and establishing your 3-Circle Plan. We are taking this responsibility off your plate so you can focus entirely on getting healthy."
  • The Pathway Back: Work with the individual and their Mentor to establish clear milestones for when they can return to serving. This return should be based on sustained periods of living within their "Outer Circle" and acting as a true "Protector of the Sandbox."
